Bludblow: Fall of the Dawn of the Rise of the Woman of Tomorrow

Strap yourself in because this is an intercoursingly good story: to atone for consorting with Satan, you've decided you to do something genuinely heroic. You soak your woollen clothes in water and create a bot which notifies you whenever a Melbourne news Twitter account tweets anything from the following list:

  • Fire
  • Inferno
  • Vesuvius 2.0
  • ... in scenes reminiscent of the underrated 1991 movie Backdraft...
  • ... more firery and dramatic than when the Globe Theatre burnt down in 1613...
  • ... burning senasation that could only be caused by skin literally being on fire...

So you know about every fire worth knowing about in Melbourne: now it's just a matter of biding your time, waiting for the perfect one to drop in on. With modern heat and smoke detectors, sprinklers and the recent trend away from cremation, people are getting better at leaving their homes before fire can kill or partially incinerate them. These are not easy times to be a hero. Few people require saving.

When you do finally decide to make your move, your car won't start so you have to take the bus to the apartment fire. To preserve your anonymity, you're wearing an eye mask you crafted by cutting eye holes in an old soccer sock. You're also wearing a costume of soaking wet merino wool thermals because you think you read one time about how wool doesn't burn. Every step you take squelches as your clothes relieve themselves of water. You ruin the bus seat with your sodden thermals plus you had to fare evade since your costume has no pocket for your Myki. When you get off the bus you set off at a steady trot in the direction of the towering inferno in the distance. It's a gate to Oblivion: the night sky is cracked with red, smoke curls around the stars, birds flee in terror. It's a gate to Fallujah: children gawp, women weep and wail, men in uniforms perspire and move around in groups. No one pays attention to the girl lumbering past them in wet thermals.

You can feel the heat on your face from a block away. This is no joke. The fire burns hot and tall in the night: it stinks of a million pieces of melted Tupperwear, the incinerated hair of a thousand dolls, the contents of a hundred freezers cooked instantly by heat hotter than any oven. A woman stranded on the 11th floor uses bed sheets and a bucket to lower her houseplants to safety. Outside the building's glowing radius, reporters crowd in the shadows. None of them wear ties or long sleeved shirts. None of them look at the fire. Make-up women rumple reporters' hair and smudge ash on their cheeks so when they shed a single sorrowful tear it'll look spectacular on camera. The fire marshal has divided displaced residents into groups based on their particular greivance: women whose babies/cats/boyfriends/vibrators/un-backed-up manuscripts are stuck in the building and possibly being consumed by flames at this very moment, people who left in such haste they forgot to grab a phone charger, and anyone who's worried the fire may be their fault because they left their box of illegal fireworks beside a faulty blow torch. This is the fall of Christendom and thousands of souls are at stake: you're gonna do your best to save a a few.

You confidently dip under the safety tape. A woman separates herself from the group of grieving women and reaches over the tape to grab the saggy elbow of your thermals. She is cartoonish in her fear: huge eyes, pink slippers, very pregnant, willing to place her faith in an unlikely hero.

She pleads: my baby is still in there.

Don't worry ma'am, I will save your precious child from this towering inferno.

You turn to leave, then she asks: don't you need to know the apartment number?

Oh yeah.

205.

Too easy.

That's the 20th floor - not the second.

I can handle it.

Be careful.

No duh, ma'am.

Your thermals are steaming before you even get through the front door. You call the elevator. When the doors creak open, smoke gasps out and you hear the ominous groaning of metal. Better take the stairs.

As you climb, the window in each fire door reveals a roving set of hellscapes: carpets completely aflame, valuables dumped in hallways by people who abandoned photo albums and parakeets to flee faster. It's a long way up. The floors aren't numbered and you lose count as you climb. Occasionally you stop for a break, sitting on the warm metal steps and sucking water from the sleeve of your thermals. You wheeze and perspire and suffer. You pass a fireman as he is descending.

Don't go up there, babe.

You lean on the hand railing and look up at him.

Whatever you left behind, you can buy a new one. There's nothing left up there. Please come down with me.

You shake your head and carry on. When you look back, you see the smoke close around him like a hug.
